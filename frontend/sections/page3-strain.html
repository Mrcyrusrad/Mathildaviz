The Strain: Mental Health and Money

What begins as financial worry soon becomes emotional fatigue. Constantly calculating how to stretch a dollar rewires the mind into survival mode.

Students under chronic cost-of-living stress describe feeling "stuck in a loop" — tired but unable to rest, busy but never caught up. Mental health data confirms what lived experience already knows: economic strain and psychological distress rise together.

Dual-Axis Line – Rent vs Distress

This chart visualizes how rent prices and mental distress rise hand-in-hand. As housing costs climb, the proportion of students reporting "very high distress" increases almost identically.

The two lines tell one story: when the cost of living goes up, mental resilience goes down. It's not just a correlation — it's causation through lived experience. Housing insecurity means emotional instability.

"It's not the assignments that break you. It's the constant stress about surviving another week." — Student testimony

Scatter Plot – Rent vs Distress Correlation

Each dot represents a city. Larger rents mean higher distress scores. The near-perfect upward slope reveals how environment shapes emotion — location becomes a mental health factor.

The data suggests that where you live determines not just your costs, but your calm.

"Geography predicts wellbeing as much as income does." — Urban wellbeing research

Bubble Chart – City Size vs Rent vs Distress

The bubble sizes emphasize scale — large, glowing circles for big cities where stress is concentrated. Students in Sydney, Melbourne, and Brisbane carry the heaviest combined burden of cost and pressure.

The smaller bubbles — Adelaide, Hobart — represent where affordability still allows breathing space. A visual metaphor: peace of mind shrinks with urban expansion.

"Peace is cheaper outside the city limits." — Regional wellbeing study

Box Plot – Sleep by Income Level

Sleep, the ultimate equalizer, becomes a privilege. Students earning less than $300 per week average under six hours of rest, while those above $500 reach eight.

Income doesn't just buy comfort — it buys mental quiet.

"Sleep inequality is the new class divide." — Sleep research study
